Company Overview

What is Robinhood?

The company that changed how America invests

🏹

The Robin Hood of Finance

Robinhood Markets, Inc. is an American financial services company headquartered in Menlo Park, California. Incorporated in 2013 and launched to the public in 2015, it operates a commission-free investing platform that democratized access to stock markets for the everyday American.

The company's name is inspired by the legendary outlaw Robin Hood, who stole from the rich to give to the poor — symbolizing their mission to take sophisticated financial tools that were once only available to the wealthy and institutional investors, and put them in the hands of ordinary people.

Robinhood pioneered zero-commission trading, which eventually forced all major US brokerages — including Charles Schwab, E*Trade, and TD Ameritrade — to eliminate their own fees by 2019, fundamentally reshaping the entire brokerage industry.

01 · The Friction

A Market Built for the Wealthy Few

The specific problem that existed before Robinhood — and who felt it most

🚧

The Problem: Investing Had a Price Tag Only the Rich Could Afford

Before 2013, buying stock in the United States meant paying a legacy broker like Charles Schwab, E*Trade, or TD Ameritrade a flat commission of $7–$10 per trade — regardless of whether you were buying $50 or $50,000 worth of shares. Many brokers also demanded account minimums of $500–$2,000 just to open a door that Wall Street insiders walked through for free.

The people who felt this most acutely were young, first-time, and low-income investors — students, hourly workers, and recent graduates who had $50 or $100 to invest but watched a third of it disappear in fees before they'd even bought a share. For them, the math simply didn't work: a $10 commission on a $100 trade is a 10% tax on entry, so most simply stayed out of the market entirely.

The founders saw the other side of that gap up close. While building high-frequency trading software for hedge funds, Vlad Tenev and Baiju Bhatt watched institutional trading desks pay fractions of a cent per trade — while ordinary Americans a few miles away were quoted $10. That asymmetry, not a business-school case study, was the spark.

Founder Story

The Two Immigrants Who Took on Wall Street

From Bulgarian and Indian roots to a $32 billion empire

Vlad Tenev

Co-Founder & CEO
🇧🇬 Born in Bulgaria, raised in Virginia, USA

Vladislav Tenev was born in Varna, Bulgaria, and moved to the United States as a child when his parents — both economists — relocated to work for the World Bank in Washington D.C. Growing up in suburban Virginia, he excelled academically before heading to Stanford.

At Stanford, he studied mathematics and physics, where he met his future co-founder. After graduation, he pursued a mathematics PhD at UCLA before dropping out to co-found his first startup with Baiju Bhatt.

Prior to Robinhood, Vlad and Baiju co-founded Celeris and Chronos Research — companies that sold high-frequency trading software to hedge funds and Wall Street institutions. This gave them deep insight into the massive gap between institutional trading costs (near zero) and retail investor costs ($5–$10 per trade).

"We built trading systems for hedge funds and saw firsthand that the financial industry was designed for the wealthy. We wanted to change that."
— Vlad Tenev

Baiju Bhatt

Co-Founder & Former Co-CEO
🇮🇳 Indian origin, raised in Virginia, USA

Baiju Bhatt was born in the United States to Indian immigrant parents who came to America to pursue PhDs. His parents settled in Virginia, giving him a remarkably similar background to his future co-founder — both were only children of immigrant scientists who grew up in the same state.

Baiju studied physics and mathematics at Stanford University, where he and Vlad became roommates, best friends, and late-night problem-set partners. Their bond was cemented over chess games, gym sessions, and a shared obsession with building things that matter.

After leaving Robinhood's day-to-day operations, Bhatt focused on other creative endeavors — reflecting his multidisciplinary curiosity that also shaped Robinhood's design-forward culture.

"At Stanford, you're surrounded by people building things that change the world. We wanted to democratize access to the markets — make investing as simple as sending a text."
— Baiju Bhatt

🎓 The Origin Story: From Stanford to Wall Street to Silicon Valley

01

Stanford Meeting (2005)

Vlad and Baiju meet during summer 2005 as undergraduates at Stanford University. Both children of immigrants, both studying mathematics and physics — they immediately bond over their shared worldview and curiosity.

02

Wall Street Experience (2010–2012)

After graduating, they move to New York City and launch two fintech companies selling ultra-fast trading algorithms to hedge funds. They witness firsthand that Wall Street firms pay pennies per trade while retail investors pay $10+.

03

The Occupy Wall Street Moment (2011)

The Occupy Wall Street movement inspires them. The 2008 financial crisis had exposed the wealth gap. They ask: "Why can't ordinary people access the same markets and tools as billionaires?" The seed of Robinhood is planted.

04

The Name "Robinhood" (2013)

They name the company after the legendary outlaw who redistributed wealth. Their tagline: "Democratize finance for all." They incorporate Robinhood Markets, Inc. on April 18, 2013 in Menlo Park, California.

05

Waitlist Launch (December 2013)

They launch a simple landing page with a waitlist. Within a day, over 10,000 people sign up. By the time they launch the app, nearly 1 million people are on the waiting list — validation that the world was ready.

Business Model

How Robinhood Makes Money

The "free" platform that generates billions through clever financial engineering

Robinhood's genius lies in its counterintuitive proposition: charge users nothing to trade, yet generate billions in revenue. The trick is that "free" only means free at the point of sale — the money still comes from somewhere, just not directly out of the customer's pocket for each trade.

In practice, four different parties end up paying: market makers pay Robinhood a small rebate to execute customer orders, Gold subscribers pay a monthly fee for premium features, borrowers and idle cash generate interest income (from margin loans and bank cash sweeps), and merchants pay interchange fees whenever someone swipes the Gold Card. No single stream is huge on its own — together, they add up to billions and forced the entire US brokerage industry to drop commissions to match.

Payment for Order Flow (PFOF)

Primary Revenue Stream · Who pays: Market makers

When a Robinhood user places a trade, that order is routed to a market maker (like Citadel Securities or Virtu Financial) rather than directly to a stock exchange. The market maker executes the trade and pays Robinhood a small rebate — typically fractions of a cent per share — in exchange for the right to fill that order.

While each rebate is tiny, millions of daily trades add up to hundreds of millions of dollars annually. This is the mechanism that makes "commission-free" trading possible in the first place.

User places order → Robinhood routes to market maker → Market maker pays rebate → User gets trade executed
⚠️ Controversial: Critics argue PFOF creates a conflict of interest, since Robinhood is paid more for order volume than for getting users the best price. The EU has banned PFOF outright, which is why Robinhood's European business (via Bitstamp) can't rely on this stream.

⭐

Robinhood Gold Subscription

Who pays: power users who opt into a $5/month or $50/year premium tier, in exchange for:

  • Higher APY on uninvested cash (3.35%)
  • 3% IRA match (vs 1% standard)
  • First $1,000 margin interest-free
  • Professional research (Morningstar)
  • Access to Gold Credit Card
4.2M subscribers in 2025
03
🏦

Net Interest Income

Who pays: borrowers and, indirectly, partner banks. Robinhood earns interest in three ways:

  • Margin Lending: interest charged when users borrow to trade
  • Cash Sweeps: uninvested cash parked at partner banks, Robinhood keeps the spread
  • Securities Lending: customer shares lent to short sellers for a fee
Largest segment in 2024–25
04
₿

Crypto Transaction Revenue

Who pays: crypto traders, via a spread built into the price. Robinhood earns the difference between what a buyer pays and what a seller receives on every crypto trade — no visible commission, but a markup baked into the quoted price.

$358M in Q4 2024 alone
05
💳

Gold Card Interchange & Partnerships

Who pays: merchants, not cardholders. Every time a Gold Card is swiped, the merchant pays an interchange fee to the card network, and Robinhood takes a cut. That cut funds the card's 3% unlimited cashback, plus a share of credit interest and partnership revenue.

Fastest growing segment

04 · Geography & Scale

From One Zip Code in California to the World

Where Robinhood started, where it went, and how big it got

🌍

Local Roots, Global Ambitions

Robinhood was incorporated in Menlo Park, California in 2013 and launched with a US-only waitlist that grew to nearly a million people before the app even opened. For its first decade, it was almost entirely a US retail-investing story — one app, one country, one market.

That changed from 2023 onward. Robinhood expanded into the United Kingdom (stocks and crypto), then accelerated into continental Europe by acquiring the Luxembourg-regulated crypto exchange Bitstamp for $200M in 2024, instantly giving it licensed access to EU markets and Bitstamp's existing international user base. Singapore and other Asian markets are the next stated targets.

The scale of that growth: from a 10,000-person waitlist in December 2013 to 27 million funded customers and $324 billion in platform assets by 2025 — a nearly 70% year-over-year jump in assets alone, run by a company of roughly 2,300 employees.

05 · Status Today

Still Standing — and Still Growing

Publicly traded, profitable, and expanding well beyond its trading-app roots

📊

Independent, Public, and Profitable

Robinhood is still operating and still independent — it has not been acquired, and it has not shut down. It went public on the NASDAQ under the ticker "HOOD" in July 2021 at a roughly $32 billion valuation, survived a brutal 2022 stock crash and two rounds of layoffs, and has since rebuilt into a consistently profitable company.

In 2025, Robinhood posted $4.5 billion in revenue and $1.9 billion in net income, with platform assets up 68% year-over-year — proof that the "free trading" model, once dismissed as unsustainable, can generate durable profit through interest income, subscriptions, and transaction-based revenue.

What this tells us: the friction Robinhood removed — high commissions and high minimums — is gone industry-wide, and it isn't coming back. Robinhood itself has evolved from a single-purpose trading app into a broader financial platform pushing into banking, credit cards, retirement accounts, and AI-driven trading, betting that the same underserved, younger customer base it won in 2013 will bring their entire financial life to the platform as they age into wealth.

The Lasting Impact — What Robinhood Changed for Good

$0

Eliminated Commissions Industry-Wide

By 2019, Robinhood's pressure forced every major US brokerage to drop trading fees to zero — saving American retail investors an estimated $1 billion+ per year in commissions.

50M+

New Investors Entered the Market

Studies show that tens of millions of first-time investors opened their first brokerage account on Robinhood — dramatically expanding stock market participation among underrepresented demographics.

$1

Fractional Share Revolution

By enabling fractional share investing from as little as $1, Robinhood made it possible for low-income investors to own shares of Amazon, Google, and Tesla.

📱

Mobile-First Investing Norm

Robinhood proved that investing could be as simple as checking social media. Its mobile-first approach forced the entire industry to redesign their apps for younger, smartphone-native investors.

₿

Mainstreamed Crypto for Retail

Robinhood was among the first major regulated platforms to offer free cryptocurrency trading, helping to mainstream Bitcoin and Ethereum adoption among retail investors.

🏛️

Sparked Regulatory Reform

The GameStop saga triggered Congressional hearings on PFOF, market structure, and the power of retail investors — conversations that continue to shape US financial regulation today.
